N-Queens

When I was in highschool, I braved an applied mathematics class in the dustiest computer room known to man. It was like a dungeon, where photons dared not enter lest they be immediately subsumed by the blinding blaze of the computer screens. Under the watchful eye of a chalk-dust coated old-guard punch-card programmer, we would wield lines of code like swords and wrestle with the nastiest algorithms, in our quest to emerge victorious in the realm of nerddom.

One fateful Friday afternoon, our professor issued a challenge. "How many configurations of 8 queens on a chessboard are there, where none of the regal ladies can attack each other?". With bonus points on the next test on the line, and a deadline set for Monday, I got to work. Before I reveal how I did it, can you solve the puzzle?

The first thing we might want to think about is giving the boot to any configurations that are obviously wrong, like, if all the queens are huddled up in a corner, or standing in a conga line. In other words, we need to invent a rule of thumb that lets us see only the configurations that we care about.

We can imagine assigning each queen her column, and letting her stand on the first free row from the top. If a whole column is being threatened, then we're stuck and have to remove the last piece placed and move the second-to-last to the next row down. This algorithm is called depth-first backtracking, because we prioritize placing as many queens as possible on the board. Play the animation below to watch how it works.
